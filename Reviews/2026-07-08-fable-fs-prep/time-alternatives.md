## Why EFS's ordering problem is not the usual one (read this first — it reframes every prior-art comparison)

Most of the distributed-systems literature on ordering was written to solve a problem EFS **does not have**, and to preserve a property EFS **actively rejects**. If you skip this section the prior-art survey will mislead you.

1. **EFS wants replay, not exactly-once.** Vector clocks, dotted version vectors, and most consensus machinery exist to detect and suppress duplicates and to establish *the* causal history. EFS's portability model is the opposite: the canonical use case is "Microsoft publishes config on chain A; anyone copies the exact signed records onto chain B and reads them natively as provably Microsoft's." That copy **is** a replay, and it must be *legal and identity-preserving*. This is why `order` is emphatically **not a nonce** — a nonce forbids replay; EFS's whole portability story is replay. Any borrowed mechanism that assumes "same event seen twice = anomaly to reconcile" is fighting the design.

2. **There is no global clock and — critically — no consensus round to manufacture one.** EFS records are chain-free EIP-712 Merkle-signed envelopes. A single author signs on their own device with no coordination. Two of that author's devices, offline from each other, may both sign. There is no leader, no quorum, no Lamport-style message-passing channel between the writers, because writers don't talk to each other — they just sign and let anyone relay. So the clock-sync techniques that assume a communication graph (Lamport happens-before, vector clocks) have *no edges to ride on* at write time. What EFS has instead is: a device wall-clock (untrusted), and — only once a record lands on a chain — that chain's `block.timestamp` (trusted, but per-chain and therefore non-portable).

3. **Ordering only needs to be per-author, and it was audited to need only that.** EFS reads (lens resolution, supersession/last-write-wins, revocation) never require a trustworthy *cross-author* global order. Who-said-what-first across two different authors is a trust question the lens layer answers by attester priority, not a timestamp question. This is a huge simplification: EFS does **not** need a total order or even a consistent partial order across authors. It needs, per author, "which of *my own* writes supersedes which." That is a far weaker requirement than almost every system in the survey was built for.

4. **Admission must be confluent and monotone.** The kernel's master invariant (see [[codex-envelope]]): *nothing may permanently reject what another kernel could accept, and no admission check may read revocable state.* This directly constrains the time model. Any ordering rule that says "reject this write because an earlier/later one exists" (a non-monotone, order-dependent admission) is **banned** — it would let two chains that received the same records in different orders admit different sets and never converge. This is why supersession is a *read-time* comparison (`(order, recordDigest)` last-write-wins, computed by resolvers) and never an *admission-time* rejection. A time model that can only be enforced by rejecting writes is dead on arrival here.

5. **IDs are content-addressed and time-free — on purpose, learned the hard way.** EAS baking `block.timestamp` into UIDs is the original sin this whole redesign routed around (children couldn't reference parents in one tx; IDs weren't portable). So time must stay *out* of identity derivation. `order` lives in the signed envelope struct and in the supersession comparator, but it must never enter an ID. This is a hard constraint on any richer scheme (e.g. you cannot make `order` a content hash of causal predecessors if that hash then leaks into a record ID).

Hold these five while reading the rest. The survey is useful precisely where a technique *respects* them and instructive where it *violates* them.

## The model EFS holds today (accurate, and all of it overturnable)

James and I separated three concepts on 2026-07-08. **[refined-with-James]** — this is the current lean, not a tested ruling. Please verify it against the codex docs and then feel free to reframe it entirely.

**`order`** (renamed from `seq` — the rename itself is part of the current thinking, to kill two footguns at once). One value **per signed envelope** — i.e. per signature / per batch, *not* per record. It is a portable ordering key: supersession is last-write-wins by `(order, recordDigest)`; a higher `order` wins a slot; a revoked slot reads EMPTY. Three things it is deliberately **not**: not a timestamp (its *meaning* is purely ordinal, even though it's *generated* from time), not a nonce (see replay point above), not an identity input. Generation is an SDK detail: it's minted from the device wall-clock encoded as a 64-bit TID (bit63 zero, 53-bit microseconds, 10 device/clock bits so two devices minting in the same microsecond don't collide destructively — and same-`(author, order)` different-digest is admitted as *both* with SeqCollision evidence, never a revert), and capped at roughly `now + 600s`. The cap is an anti-abuse bound: without it an author could mint `order = MAX` and win every future slot forever ("max-it-out-to-win-forever"); the cap self-regenerates as real time advances. **But the field's meaning is ordinal only** — the time-derivation is how you get a coordination-free, roughly-monotonic-per-device number, not a claim about when anything happened.

**`claimedAt`** (new, optional, **per record / per action**). The user's *untrusted* claim of when they did that specific action. It exists because of batching: one signature can cover many queued actions that share a single `order`, so if you wanted per-action authoring time you'd have lost it — the batch collapses it. `claimedAt` gives timeline/journal/blog apps an honest per-action authoring order to render, while being explicitly "the author says so, believe it at your own risk." Never used for proof, never used by the kernel. Its status is genuinely **[open]**: blessed reserved field vs. app-layer convention (the same row-vs-convention-vs-reject question the P2 reserved-key candidates face).

**`admittedAt`** (chain-stamped, **per claim**, trustworthy-but-local). `block.timestamp` at the moment the kernel admitted the record, stored as `admittedAt[claimId]` in kernel state so it's `getProof`-provable (this is the client-OS P1 "trustworthy clock" ask). It is the *only* trustworthy time in the system — but it is **per-chain**: a replica of the same record on chain B gets a *different* `admittedAt`. That non-portability is exactly why supersession orders on `order` (which travels with the signature) and not on chain-time (which doesn't). So the honest one-liner James wants ratified project-wide: *"the author-asserted timestamp is untrusted; the trustworthy clock is admission — per-chain, venue-labeled, never global; EFS reads need only per-author order."*

**The net picture as it stands:** three honest concepts — `claimedAt` (per action, user claim, untrusted), `order` (per batch, portable ordering, untrusted-as-clock), `admittedAt` (per chain, kernel-stamped, trustworthy). **Freeze-sensitivity you must respect even while overturning:** `order` lives *inside the signed envelope struct* and `claimedAt` would live inside the signed record body — both are on the format surface bound by the one-final-freeze pledge. So whatever you conclude, the *reserved shape* (is there a per-record time word? how wide? optional?) needs to converge before the ceremony even if the semantics keep iterating. That's the cross-cutting freeze rule: stage the design, but surface the freeze-sensitive slot early.

## Prior art — how others order writes without a global clock

Each entry: the mechanism, then the honest EFS read (borrow / avoid / why), tied back to the five constraints.

### Lamport clocks (happens-before, scalar)
A scalar counter bumped on every local event and on message receipt (`max(local, received)+1`). Gives a total order consistent with causality, but the order is *arbitrary* between concurrent events and requires a message channel between processes to carry the counter. **EFS read:** the *shape* of `order` (a scalar, roughly-monotone-per-writer, tie-broken deterministically by `recordDigest`) is a Lamport-flavored idea and it's a good fit for the per-author requirement. But classic Lamport assumes writers exchange messages so counters stay coupled — EFS writers don't (constraint 2). EFS substitutes the device wall-clock for the "receive a higher counter" step, which is exactly what HLC formalizes (below). Borrow the scalar-total-order instinct; don't assume the message channel exists.

### Vector clocks
One counter per writer; compare element-wise to get a true partial order and *detect* concurrency. The gold standard for "did these two writes causally conflict?" **EFS read:** mostly a **trap** here, for three reasons. (1) Vector clocks exist to detect and reconcile concurrent conflicting writes — EFS deliberately does *not* reconcile; it admits both and resolves at read time by `(order, recordDigest)` LWW + lens attester-priority (constraint 4). (2) They grow with the number of writers, which is unbounded and permissionless in EFS — an O(writers) metadata blob per record is a non-starter for a 100-year archive with anyone able to write. (3) They need the writer set to be known/bounded. The *one* place a vector-clock idea could earn its keep is a bounded, opt-in, per-*document* multi-writer collaboration primitive (the Pass-1 "wiki with 5 editors" surface) — but note CRDTs were already dismissed early and the shared-folder story is explicitly flagged as needing a fresh check, so if you resurrect any per-writer causal metadata, do it as a scoped app-layer/LIST-charter feature, not a kernel field.

### Hybrid Logical Clocks (HLC) — the closest living relative of `order`
HLC combines physical time with a logical counter: the timestamp is `max(physical_now, received_hlc) + logical_tiebreak`, bounded to stay close to wall-clock. You get timestamps that are (a) monotonic, (b) close to real time (so human-meaningful and comparable across nodes without sync), (c) causally consistent where a channel exists, and (d) a single compact scalar. **EFS read:** this is essentially what EFS's `order` *is* structurally — a physical-time-derived, bounded (`now+600s`), tie-broken (10 device bits + `recordDigest`) scalar. The most useful thing you can do here is **name it as HLC-family and steal HLC's rigor**: HLC has a well-studied bound-drift analysis, a clean story for why the physical component can't run away, and known failure modes (clock skew beyond the bound, the "stuck logical counter" pathology). EFS's `now+600s` cap is a cruder version of HLC's bounded-drift guarantee. **Question worth putting to the literature:** does HLC's bound analysis give a principled value for the 600s cap and the 10-bit device field, instead of the current hand-picked numbers? And does HLC's causal-consistency-where-a-channel-exists buy EFS anything on a *single author's* multiple devices (which is the only place two writes race)?

### Interval Tree Clocks (ITC)
A causality mechanism designed for dynamic, uncoordinated populations where nodes fork and join without a global registry — the id-space splits and merges. Built precisely for "processes come and go and nobody assigns them numbers." **EFS read:** intellectually the closest match to EFS's *permissionless* writer model (no bounded, known writer set — the thing that kills vector clocks). Almost certainly still **too heavy** to sit in the record body, and it again solves a conflict-detection problem EFS routes around at read time. But worth a look for one narrow thing: the **10 device bits** in the TID are a fixed, collision-prone allocation of the same "which of my devices signed this" problem ITC solves elegantly. If multi-device authorship ever needs to be more than "hope 10 bits is enough," ITC's fork/join id-space is the reference design. Likely reserve-not-build, like KEL.

### Dotted Version Vectors (DVV)
A refinement of vector clocks (Riak's answer to sibling explosion) that tracks per-write "dots" so you can represent concurrent versions compactly without unbounded growth. **EFS read:** same family, same core mismatch (it's for reconciling concurrent siblings). The transferable idea is *representing multiple live versions of one key without picking a winner at write time* — which rhymes with EFS's "admit both, resolve at read." If Pass-1 versioning/history wants a "show me all concurrent heads of this file" view, DVV's compact-siblings representation is prior art for the *read-side* view, not a kernel field.

### CRDT causal metadata
CRDTs carry per-operation causal context (often vector-clock-ish or dotted) so replicas converge deterministically without coordination. **EFS read:** EFS is *already* a coarse CRDT — the G-set revocation is literally a grow-only set, LWW-per-slot is an LWW-register, and admission is confluent by the master invariant. That's not an accident; it's the same convergence math. So the honest framing is: **EFS deliberately chose the cheapest CRDT primitives (G-set, LWW-register) and pushed everything else to read-time lenses, specifically to avoid carrying rich causal metadata in a permanent archive.** The open question is whether any Pass-1 filesystem feature (multi-writer merge, move-with-history) genuinely needs a richer CRDT (an RGA/sequence-CRDT for collaborative text, say) — and if so, whether that lives above the kernel entirely. The lean is "keep the kernel a trivial CRDT; richer convergence is app-layer" but that has not been tested against the wiki case.

### Nostr `created_at`
Nostr events carry a Unix `created_at` set by the client — untrusted, used for display ordering and for relay replaceable-event resolution ("keep the newest per kind+pubkey"). It's famous for its footgun: clients set it, so it's spoofable, and relays that use it for "latest wins" can be gamed by future-dating. **EFS read:** this is the **cautionary twin of `order` + `claimedAt`**, and it's the single most relevant real-world deployment to study. Nostr conflated "display time" and "which-is-latest ordering" into one untrusted client field and got exactly the future-dating exploit EFS's `now+600s` cap is trying to pre-empt. EFS's *separation* of `order` (ordinal, capped) from `claimedAt` (display, honestly-untrusted) is arguably the fix Nostr wishes it had — but you should read how Nostr relays actually handle future-dated events (many clamp or reject) and whether EFS's cap is the right clamp. Nostr's replaceable-event model (kind+pubkey → keep newest) is also a direct analogue of EFS per-slot LWW; their edge cases are your edge cases.

### ATProto TID and `rev`
Bluesky's AT Protocol uses **TIDs** (timestamp identifiers: microsecond clock + a random/clock-id component, base32-sorted) as record keys, and a repo **`rev`** (also a TID) that must monotonically increase per commit to signal "this version is newer" to relays/the firehose. **EFS read:** this is a strikingly close cousin — EFS's `order` is *literally the same construction* (microseconds + clock-id bits in a 64-bit sortable key). Two things to mine: (1) ATProto learned that `rev` must be *monotonic per repo* for the firehose to order commits, and has a spec for what happens on clock regression — directly relevant to whether EFS's per-envelope `order` needs a monotonicity guarantee or is content to be roughly-monotone. (2) ATProto keeps TIDs as *record keys* (identity), which EFS forbids (constraint 5) — so study *why* they can and EFS can't, because it sharpens the "time out of identity" boundary. The divergence is as instructive as the similarity.

### Farcaster ordering
Farcaster Hubs order messages by a per-message timestamp plus a tie-break, and use CRDT-style set reconciliation (add/remove sets with LWW) across hubs to converge. **EFS read:** Farcaster independently arrived at *the same shape as EFS* — timestamped messages + LWW/G-set convergence across replicas with no global clock. That convergence is real-world evidence the EFS approach works at scale; their reconciliation protocol and their handling of timestamp bounds (Farcaster clamps timestamps to a window too) are worth reading as a second data point next to Nostr. Note both Farcaster and EFS use a *bounded* timestamp — the bound is load-bearing in both, which suggests it's a genuine pattern and not an EFS hack.

### git's DAG (Merkle causal history)
git orders commits by an explicit parent-pointer DAG, not by time — commit timestamps are decorative and famously unreliable; the *structure* (each commit hashes its parents) is the source of truth. **EFS read:** this is the sharpest challenge to EFS's current stance, and it's where `prev` comes in. EFS *has* a `prev` field in the envelope but **hard-fences it as signed evidence + replication hint, never read by any admission rule** (constraint 4 — a causal-parent check would be non-monotone). git demonstrates the opposite philosophy: make the causal DAG *the* ordering primitive and let time be decorative. **The open question this raises (surfaced below) is whether EFS is right to fence `prev` to evidence-only, or whether a git-like content-addressed causal chain should be a first-class, opt-in ordering signal** for apps that want tamper-evident history (versioning, the Pass-1 "restore v3" surface). The tension is real: a `prev`-DAG gives you provable history and cheap "is X an ancestor of Y" **but** any *admission-time* use of it breaks confluence, and any *read-time* use is just an app convention you can already build on the existing evidence-only `prev`. Pressure this. git is the existence proof that time can be almost entirely decorative if the DAG carries the order.

### Datomic — tx-time vs valid-time (bitemporality) — *name the model well*
Datomic records every fact with a **transaction time** (when the DB learned it, system-assigned, immutable, the basis of `as-of` queries) and supports **valid time** (when the fact was true in the world, user-asserted) as a separate axis. This is **bitemporality**, a decades-old database concept (SQL:2011 has it too): two orthogonal time axes — *when did the system record this* vs *when was it true*. **EFS read — this is probably the most valuable single lens in this document.** Map it:

- `admittedAt` **is transaction time** (when *this chain* recorded the fact; system-assigned; immutable per chain).
- `claimedAt` **is valid time** (when the author says the thing was true/done; user-asserted; the bitemporal "valid time" axis exactly).
- `order` is a **third thing** that bitemporality doesn't have a clean name for: it's neither — it's a *portable supersession key*. It's closest to a **`rev` / logical version stamp** (ATProto's word) or a **write-order sequence** that happens to be time-derived. Calling it "time" at all is arguably the naming bug; it's an *ordering* stamp.

**Why this matters:** giving EFS's three fields their proper bitemporal names ("valid time" / "transaction time" / "revision-order key") (a) instantly tells any database-literate developer what each is for, (b) surfaces that `order` is doing double duty — it's the LWW comparator *and* a proxy for "authoring order," and bitemporality suggests those might want to be different axes, and (c) connects EFS to a large, well-understood body of query semantics (`as-of`, `as-at`, temporal joins) that a 100-year archive will eventually want. **The question to put to Fable:** is the clean model actually *bitemporal* (valid-time = `claimedAt`, transaction-time = `admittedAt`) with `order` demoted to "the portable revision key that exists only because transaction-time isn't portable"? That reframing might be cleaner than "three time fields," because it says clearly: EFS has the two canonical temporal axes plus **one portability patch** (`order`) that exists solely because `admittedAt` can't travel across chains. If cross-chain transaction-time *could* travel (it can't, honestly — that's the no-cross-chain-currency ruling), `order` might not need to exist at all. That's a deep insight worth chasing or refuting.

## Open questions to surface (not to decide)

These are the surfaces I most want Fable to reframe. None has a preferred answer here; each has a real tension.

1. **Per-envelope vs per-record `order`.** Today `order` is one value per signature (per batch), and `claimedAt` was *invented specifically to recover per-action granularity* that batching destroys. That's a smell: we added a second field to patch the granularity the first field threw away. Is the clean design a per-*record* ordinal (making `claimedAt` partly redundant), accepting the cost (more signed bytes, and you lose the "one batch = one atomic ordering event" property that makes cross-chain replication clean)? Or is per-batch correct and `claimedAt` genuinely a different axis (valid-time) that would exist regardless? The bitemporal lens suggests the latter — but test it.

2. **Is a single scalar enough, or is causal/prev-linkage ever needed?** EFS fenced `prev` to evidence-only to protect admission confluence (constraint 4). But git shows a causal DAG can carry ordering that a scalar can't (true concurrency detection, provable ancestry, tamper-evident history). The Pass-1 versioning/history/undo surface ("restore yesterday's version," `supersededBy` chains) is exactly where a scalar LWW might be too weak and a DAG too heavy. Options in the space: (a) keep `prev` evidence-only, build history as an app convention on top; (b) bless a *read-time-only* causal-parent semantic (apps that opt in get provable history; kernel still never reads it, so confluence holds); (c) something richer. The fence is protective, not sacred — pressure whether "evidence-only" is leaving a genuinely useful, confluence-safe primitive on the table.

3. **`claimedAt`: blessed reserved field vs app convention.** If it's a blessed field in the signed body, it's freeze-sensitive (reserve the slot now or never), it's uniform across apps (timelines interoperate), and it's honestly-labeled-untrusted in one place. If it's an app convention (a reserved-key string property, say), it costs nothing to defer and stays maximally flexible, but every journal app reinvents it and they don't interoperate. This is the identical row-vs-convention-vs-reject question the P2 reserved-key candidates face — decide it *with* them, not separately.

4. **Is there a cleaner unification of the three concepts?** The current three-field model is a *conversation artifact* — we found the three by pulling on "batching loses per-action time." A from-scratch designer might land on two (valid-time + transaction-time, à la Datomic, with the revision-order falling out of one of them) or on four (if multi-device authorship needs an explicit device/causality axis). The bitemporal framing is the most promising unification candidate; ITC/HLC suggest a possible device-axis fourth. Reframe freely.

5. **What does bitemporality actually buy a 100-year archive?** SQL:2011 and Datomic support bitemporal queries because auditors, historians, and correction-handling need "what did we believe on date X about what was true on date Y." A 100-year credibly-neutral archive is *the* setting where that pays off (retroactive corrections, provenance disputes, "what was the record as-of the 2074 fork"). But it also has costs (two time axes to store, index, and reason about; more surface to get wrong at freeze). Is the archive better served by fully embracing bitemporality now (reserve both axes cleanly, name them, spec the `as-of` read semantics) or by shipping the minimum (transaction-time + a portable order) and letting valid-time be convention? The answer shapes what must be reserved before freeze.

6. **The generation mechanics deserve their own scrutiny** (secondary, but freeze-adjacent). The 600s cap and 10 device bits are hand-picked. HLC's bounded-drift analysis and ITC's fork/join id-space are the principled references. Is 600s right? Is 10 bits enough collision resistance for a heavy multi-device author over 100 years? What's the actual failure mode when the cap clamps a legitimately-future-dated write (does the write silently get an earlier `order` than the author intended, and does that ever flip a supersession)? Nostr and Farcaster both clamp — study what breaks when they do.

## What "going wider" could look like (invitation, not instruction)

If none of the above is the real frontier, some directions that would be *more* than we asked for and welcome: question whether "time" is the right frame at all (git says it's decorative; maybe EFS's honest model is "one portable ordering key + two optional untrusted human-time annotations, and we should stop calling any of it a clock"); investigate whether the no-cross-chain-currency ruling and the existence of `order` are the same fact wearing two hats (if transaction-time could travel, does `order` evaporate?); or design the read-side temporal query surface a 100-year archive will want (`as-of` a block, `as-of` an author-order, valid-time-range) and let *that* pull the field requirements, rather than designing fields first. The mission ends are the only fence. Everything else is yours to redraw.
